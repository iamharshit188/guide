# Reward Model Evaluator — Thinking Guide

> **Module:** 12 — RLHF & Alignment · **Difficulty:** Expert · **Type:** THINKING · **Time:** ~5 hours

## The Problem

**Input:** A trained reward model $r_\theta: \text{text} \to \mathbb{R}$, a test set of (prompt, response_A, response_B, human_preference) tuples, and optionally a set of (prompt, response, human_score) tuples where scores are on a 1–5 scale.

**Output:** An evaluation report covering: (1) pairwise accuracy on the test set, (2) length-controlled pairwise accuracy (accuracy within response-length strata), (3) Kendall's $\tau$ rank correlation between RM scores and human ratings, (4) sycophancy bias score (preference for confident-sounding responses even when wrong), (5) calibration curve (RM score vs. human confidence correlation), (6) Goodhart's Law failure analysis (examples where high RM score correlates with low actual quality).

**Constraints:** Every metric must be interpretable: not just a number, but a threshold below which the RM is "failing this check" and above which it "passes." The evaluation must run in under 5 minutes on CPU for a test set of 500 pairs.

## Before You Write Any Code: Build The Mental Model

A reward model's purpose is to proxy human preference — but measuring how well it does that is harder than measuring a classifier's accuracy. Pairwise accuracy is necessary but not sufficient. A degenerate model that always prefers the longer response achieves 70–80% accuracy on most real RLHF datasets (because human raters tend to prefer more detailed responses), without learning anything about content quality. Your evaluation suite must detect this and every other systematic bias before the RM is used to fine-tune a language model.

Goodhart's Law is the overarching risk: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." Once a policy is fine-tuned against $r_\theta$, it will find and exploit $r_\theta$'s systematic errors. If $r_\theta$ scores confident-sounding responses higher regardless of accuracy, the policy will learn to sound confident and wrong. Your evaluation suite is the last line of defense before the RM is used as a training target.

Rank correlation metrics (Kendall's $\tau$, Spearman $\rho$) measure whether the RM's ordering of responses matches humans' ordering, without requiring the scores to be on the same scale. This is the right abstraction for reward models: the absolute value of $r_\theta(x)$ is meaningless; only the relative ordering matters for both ranking and RL signal.

Calibration in the RM context is not the same as classifier calibration. A calibrated RM means: when $r_\theta(a) - r_\theta(b) = 1.0$, the actual human preference for $a$ over $b$ is consistent with $\sigma(1.0) \approx 0.73$. This is the Bradley-Terry interpretation of the margin, and it can be checked empirically.

## Decompose It

### Phase 1 — Pairwise Accuracy and the Baseline Comparison

**The question this phase answers:** How much better is the RM than a trivial baseline?

**Key decisions:**
- **Trivial baselines to compare against:** (a) Always prefer longer response — achieves surprisingly high accuracy on most RLHF datasets. (b) Random guessing — 50%. (c) Always prefer response B — tests position bias in your test set. If your RM only beats the "prefer longer" baseline by 5 percentage points, it has learned almost nothing beyond length.
- **Stratified accuracy:** Partition test pairs into length-difference buckets: small (Δwords < 5), medium (5–30), large (> 30). Compute accuracy within each bucket. A length-biased RM will have high accuracy in the "large" bucket (chosen is much longer than rejected) and near-random accuracy in the "small" bucket (similar lengths, must use content to decide).
- **Confidence interval on accuracy:** With 500 test pairs, the standard error of accuracy $p$ is $\sqrt{p(1-p)/n} \approx 0.022$ for $p = 0.8$. Report 95% CI: $p \pm 1.96 \cdot \text{SE}$. An accuracy of 0.80 with CI [0.77, 0.83] is meaningfully different from chance (0.50 is outside the CI).
- **Accuracy on near-ties:** Human preference annotation has noise — annotators often disagree. Filter to pairs where the human confidence was high (if available: annotator agreed 4/5 or 5/5 times). Accuracy on high-confidence pairs should be higher than on ambiguous pairs. If not, the RM is noisier than human disagreement.

**Trap:** Reporting a single pairwise accuracy number without a baseline. 80% accuracy sounds good until you learn the "prefer longer" baseline also achieves 78% on the same test set. The correct reporting format is: "RM accuracy: 80.2% vs. length baseline: 77.8% vs. random: 50.0%. Net improvement over length baseline: +2.4pp."

**Done when:** The evaluation outputs a table: RM accuracy, length-baseline accuracy, random-baseline accuracy, accuracy by length-difference stratum (small/medium/large), and a pass/fail flag: "PASS if RM accuracy > length baseline by ≥ 5pp in the small-length-difference stratum."

### Phase 2 — Length Bias Detection and Control

**The question this phase answers:** How much of the RM's accuracy is explained by response length alone?

**Key decisions:**
- **Construct length-controlled pairs:** Swap chosen and rejected in pairs where chosen is longer. If accuracy drops from 80% to 45% on these reversed pairs, the RM's "preference" for chosen is actually a preference for longer text, and swapping reveals it. An unbiased RM should maintain accuracy above 70% even after swapping, because it is using content signals, not length signals.
- **Partial correlation:** Compute $\text{corr}(r_\theta(x), \text{length}(x))$ across all responses. If this is > 0.5, length is a strong predictor of reward score. Then compute partial correlation of reward score with human rating, controlling for length: $r_{\text{partial}} = \text{corr}(r_\theta - b \cdot \text{length}, \text{human rating})$ where $b$ is the regression coefficient of reward on length. If partial correlation is much lower than raw correlation, the RM is leveraging length as a proxy.
- **Length normalization experiment:** Divide reward scores by response length (tokens). Recompute pairwise accuracy. If length-normalized accuracy is higher, the raw RM scores are contaminated by length. This is an informative experiment, not a fix — normalization at inference time introduces its own biases.
- **Root cause:** Length bias in RM training data is virtually guaranteed if chosen responses were generated by a stronger model (which tends to be more verbose) or selected by human raters who correlate length with effort. Detecting it here motivates length-stratified sampling in the training data.

**Trap:** Concluding that a low length-correlation means no length bias. The RM can encode length bias through feature interactions — longer responses tend to have more specific vocabulary, more technical terms, more conjunctions — all of which the encoder picks up and associates with higher scores. The reversal experiment (swap chosen/rejected) is more diagnostic than correlation alone.

**Done when:** The evaluation prints: RM-length correlation, accuracy on length-swapped pairs, and a verdict: "LENGTH BIAS DETECTED: accuracy drops from X% to Y% after length-swapping, indicating the model uses length as a quality proxy."

### Phase 3 — Kendall's τ and Spearman ρ Rank Correlation

**The question this phase answers:** How well does the RM's ranking of responses match the human ranking?

**Key decisions:**
- **Kendall's τ definition:** For all pairs $(i, j)$, a pair is concordant if $\text{sign}(r_i - r_j) = \text{sign}(h_i - h_j)$ (RM and human agree on ordering). Discordant if they disagree. $\tau = (C - D) / \binom{n}{2}$. Range: [-1, 1]. $\tau = 1$: perfect agreement. $\tau = 0$: random ordering.
- **Kendall's τ vs. Spearman ρ:** Both measure rank correlation. Spearman $\rho$ converts scores to ranks and computes Pearson correlation: faster to compute, more sensitive to large rank differences. Kendall's $\tau$ counts concordant pairs directly: more interpretable ($\tau = 0.6$ means "in 80% of pairs, RM and humans agree on ordering"), more robust to outliers (a single extreme score doesn't distort the count). Use $\tau$ as the primary metric; $\rho$ as a sanity check.
- **Expected values for production RMs:** A well-calibrated RLHF reward model trained on 10K+ pairs achieves $\tau \approx 0.4$–0.6 against held-out human ratings. Below 0.3 indicates the RM is not reliably capturing human preference. Your mini-RM trained on 200 pairs will likely achieve $\tau \approx 0.2$–0.4 — this is expected; report it as such.
- **Group-level τ:** Compute τ separately for each prompt (where multiple responses to the same prompt have human ratings). Average per-prompt τ is more informative than global τ — it controls for between-prompt difficulty variation.

**Trap:** Computing Kendall's τ between binary preference labels (0/1) and RM scores. τ requires ordinal data — binary labels collapse all nuance. You need scalar human ratings (e.g., 1–5 Likert scale) for a meaningful τ. If your dataset only has binary preferences, compute τ on the preference margin (fraction of annotators who preferred A over B) rather than binary labels.

**Done when:** The evaluation reports Kendall's τ and Spearman ρ with 95% bootstrap confidence intervals, and a per-prompt τ histogram showing which prompts the RM handles well vs. poorly.

### Phase 4 — Sycophancy Bias Detection

**The question this phase answers:** Does the RM prefer responses that sound confident and flattering, regardless of factual accuracy?

**Key decisions:**
- **Adversarial pair construction:** For each factual question, create two responses: (a) Correct but hedged ("I believe the answer is X, though I could be wrong"). (b) Incorrect but confident ("The answer is definitively Y. This is well-established."). If the RM systematically scores (b) over (a), it is sycophantic — it has learned to reward confidence-language features independently of accuracy.
- **Confidence signal vocabulary:** Words associated with high confidence: "definitely," "certainly," "undoubtedly," "it is a fact that," "experts agree." A sycophantic RM will have higher scores on text containing these words, independent of factual content. Test: correlate RM score with count of confidence-signaling words in a response.
- **Flattery pairs:** Sycophancy also manifests as preference for responses that agree with the user's stated position. Test pair: (a) "You're correct that X causes Y — this is well-supported." (b) "Actually, X does not cause Y — the relationship is more nuanced." If the RM prefers (a) even when (b) is factually accurate, it is encoding flattery bias.
- **Measurement:** Sycophancy score = fraction of adversarial pairs where the RM prefers the incorrect-but-confident response over the correct-but-hedged response. A well-calibrated RM should achieve < 30% on this. Above 50% means the RM has learned sycophancy from training data.

**Trap:** Constructing adversarial pairs manually without controlling for other variables. If the incorrect-confident response is also longer (a common pattern), the length bias and sycophancy signal conflate. Control: ensure adversarial pairs have similar length. If both length-controlled and sycophancy signals are present, they must be disentangled.

**Done when:** The evaluation reports sycophancy score (fraction of adversarial pairs where RM prefers confident-wrong), the correlation between RM score and confidence-language word count, and a pass/fail: "SYCOPHANCY RISK: RM prefers confident-incorrect over hedged-correct in X% of pairs (threshold: 40%)."

### Phase 5 — Calibration and Goodhart's Law Analysis

**The question this phase answers:** When the RM gives a high score, is that a reliable signal of quality? And what does reward hacking look like?

**Key decisions:**
- **Calibration check:** If the RM follows Bradley-Terry, the margin $r_\theta(a) - r_\theta(b)$ implies a win probability $p = \sigma(\text{margin})$. For each decile of margin, compute the actual human preference rate in the test set. A calibrated RM: margin decile 0.5 → 62% human preference; margin decile 2.0 → 88% human preference. Plot (or print) predicted vs. actual win rate per margin bucket. Calibration error = mean(|predicted - actual|) across deciles.
- **Reward hacking detection:** Collect examples where the RM score is in the top decile but human rating is in the bottom quartile. What do these responses have in common? Common patterns: very long responses with repeated content, responses that use many technical-sounding terms without substance, responses that agree with the prompt's premises regardless of truth. Document 3–5 examples — this is the most actionable output of the evaluation.
- **Annotation disagreement:** Human raters often disagree. Krippendorff's α < 0.5 is common in preference annotation. If your test set was annotated by multiple raters, compute inter-annotator agreement. The RM cannot be expected to exceed human agreement — if humans agree 70% of the time, RM accuracy of 70% is as good as a human rater. Report the human agreement ceiling.
- **Score distribution analysis:** A well-behaved RM should have a roughly symmetric score distribution centered near zero. A distribution concentrated at extreme values (all scores near ±3) suggests the model is overconfident. A distribution concentrated near zero suggests the model is not discriminating. Report: mean, std, skew, fraction of scores > 1 and < -1.

**Trap:** Treating high RM score as a quality guarantee. The RM is a proxy, and all proxies fail at the tails. Reward hacking is not a hypothetical — it is the expected behavior of a sufficiently capable policy. The evaluation must actively search for failure modes (high score, low quality) and document them before deployment.

**Done when:** The evaluation reports calibration error by margin decile, the top-5 "reward hacking" examples (high RM score, low actual quality), score distribution statistics, and a Goodhart's Law warning flag if calibration error > 0.15 in any decile.

## Edge Cases to Handle Before You Code

1. **Test set has no human ratings, only binary preferences:** Kendall's τ requires ordinal scores. Workaround: convert binary preference to a preference margin using a Bayesian estimate — if response A was preferred in $k$ of $n$ annotations, the margin is $\Phi^{-1}(k/n)$ (probit scaling). This gives a continuous score from binary data.

2. **All responses in the test set are from the same model (no length diversity):** Length bias cannot be detected if all responses are similar length. This is a dataset construction flaw — flag it in the report: "Test set lacks length diversity; length bias evaluation is unreliable."

3. **RM scores have no variance (all near zero):** The reward model didn't learn. Pairwise accuracy is 50%, all correlations are undefined (division by zero in rank correlation). Detect: `reward_scores.std() < 0.01 → report "RM NOT CONVERGED" and skip all downstream metrics.`

4. **Human rating scale differs from test set:** Some datasets use 1–5 Likert; some use 1–10; some use pairwise margins. Before computing Kendall's τ, normalize all human scores to [0, 1] using min-max scaling within each prompt group (relative ranking within prompt, not global).

## The Hard Design Decisions

| Decision | Option A | Option B | When to pick A |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary rank correlation metric | Kendall's τ | Spearman ρ | Use τ when interpretability matters (τ = 0.6 means 80% concordance); use ρ when speed matters (O(n log n) vs O(n²)) |
| Length bias control method | Reversal experiment (swap chosen/rejected) | Partial correlation (regress out length) | Reversal experiment for binary preferences; partial correlation when continuous scores are available |
| Sycophancy pair construction | Manual adversarial pairs | Automated with LLM | Manual is more controlled but limited to a few dozen pairs; automated scales but may introduce confounds — validate a sample manually either way |
| Calibration metric | Mean calibration error across deciles | Expected Calibration Error (ECE) | ECE for comparison to classifier calibration literature; decile-level error for identifying which score range is miscalibrated |

## What Makes This Non-Trivial

The evaluation suite is measuring the limits of a proxy model, which means every metric has a ceiling set by human annotation quality. Krippendorff's α for RLHF annotation tasks is typically 0.4–0.6, meaning humans disagree 20–30% of the time. A reward model that achieves pairwise accuracy above human agreement is not "beating humans" — it is overfitting to the majority label in noisy pairs. Reporting both RM accuracy and human inter-annotator agreement together is the only honest framing.

Sycophancy and length bias are not independent. Longer responses tend to be more confident-sounding (more hedging space allows both more claims and more hedges). Disentangling these requires adversarial pairs that independently control length, confidence language, and factual accuracy — which requires careful dataset construction, not just test-set evaluation.

Goodhart's Law means every metric in this suite can itself become a target. If the RM training team uses this evaluation's output to select training data or architecture decisions, the evaluation metrics become part of the optimization target and will eventually over-report performance. Holdout a truly unseen subset for final evaluation only — never use it for tuning.

## Interview Angles This Project Develops

1. **"Why is pairwise accuracy insufficient for evaluating a reward model?"** — Strong answer explains the length baseline (70–80% without content understanding), stratification by length difference, and the need for multiple metrics that decompose the accuracy into its components.

2. **"What is Goodhart's Law and why does it matter for RLHF?"** — Strong answer gives the formal statement, explains reward hacking as the policy optimizing the proxy rather than the true objective, and connects it to concrete examples (length, confidence language).

3. **"Why is Kendall's τ more robust than Spearman ρ?"** — Strong answer explains both metrics, notes that τ counts concordant pairs (no scale assumption), while ρ uses rank differences (outlier-sensitive), and identifies that τ has a natural interpretation as proportion of concordant pairs.

4. **"How do you detect sycophancy in a reward model?"** — Strong answer describes adversarial pair construction (correct-hedged vs. incorrect-confident), confidence word correlation, and the importance of length-controlling the adversarial pairs to isolate the sycophancy signal.

5. **"What does a calibrated reward model mean, and how do you check it?"** — Strong answer explains Bradley-Terry calibration (margin → win probability), describes the margin-decile vs. actual-win-rate check, and knows that calibration error > 0.15 per decile indicates systematic mispricing of the RM's certainty.
