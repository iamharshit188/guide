# Bayesian Spam Classifier from Scratch — Thinking Guide

> **Module:** 01 — Math for ML · **Difficulty:** Intermediate · **Type:** THINKING · **Time:** ~3 hours

## The Problem

**Input:** A labeled dataset of emails — each email is a raw string, each label is `spam` or `ham`. The dataset is split into train and test sets.

**Output:** A trained Naive Bayes classifier that, given any new email string, outputs (a) a classification (`spam` or `ham`) and (b) the log-probability of each class. Evaluation: precision, recall, F1, and accuracy on the test set, printed to terminal.

**Constraints:** No sklearn in the core probabilistic pipeline. Tokenization, count computation, Laplace smoothing, and classification must be implemented from scratch. `collections.Counter` is allowed. The 20 Newsgroups dataset (`sklearn.datasets.fetch_20newsgroups`) is allowed purely for data loading.

## Before You Write Any Code: Build The Mental Model

Naive Bayes is Bayes' theorem with one critical simplification. Bayes says:

$$P(C \mid \mathbf{x}) \propto P(\mathbf{x} \mid C) \cdot P(C)$$

The likelihood $P(\mathbf{x} \mid C)$ is the probability of observing the entire email given the class. For an email with words $[w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_n]$, this is a joint distribution over all word positions — intractable without structure. Naive Bayes assumes conditional independence: given the class $C$, each word $w_i$ is drawn independently from the same class-conditional distribution.

$$P(\mathbf{x} \mid C) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(w_i \mid C)$$

This is "naive" because words in emails are obviously not independent (spam emails love the phrase "click here now" as a unit, not as three independent draws). The independence assumption is wrong, and yet the classifier works surprisingly well in practice. Why? Because even with wrong probability estimates, the *ranking* of classes is usually correct — the product of class-conditional probabilities is dominated by the most discriminative words, and those dominate regardless of the wrong independence assumption.

The computation requires log-space arithmetic and smoothed probabilities — both of which are design necessities, not implementation details.

## Decompose It

### Phase 1 — Tokenization and Vocabulary Construction

**The question this phase answers:** How do you transform raw email strings into a representation the probabilistic model can use?

**Key decisions:**
- **Tokenization strategy:** The simplest correct approach is `text.lower().split()` — lowercase and whitespace-split. This is fast and produces reasonable tokens. More principled alternatives: (a) regex-based `re.findall(r'\b[a-z]+\b', text.lower())` removes punctuation and numbers; (b) character n-grams capture subword patterns; (c) stemming/lemmatization merges "running"/"runs"/"run" into one token. For spam classification, simple whitespace-split plus lowercasing is sufficient. Don't over-engineer tokenization before verifying the model works at all.
- **Vocabulary cutoff:** Include all words seen in training data? Or only words seen at least $k$ times? The long tail of rare words (seen once or twice) adds noise and memory cost without contributing signal. A minimum count threshold of 2–5 eliminates typos and rare proper nouns that would otherwise inflate the vocabulary. Vocabulary size strongly affects memory and inference speed.
- **Vocabulary leakage:** The vocabulary must be built from training data *only*. At test time, words not in the vocabulary are silently ignored (unknown words add zero log-probability contribution). This is correct — the model has no information about unseen words.

**Trap:** Including test data in vocabulary construction. If a word appears only in test data but your vocabulary contains it (because you built vocab from all data), you will compute non-trivial probabilities for it during test-time classification, even though the model was never trained on it. Always `fit` on train, `transform` on test.

**Done when:** A tokenized training corpus produces a vocabulary dict `{word: index}` with vocabulary size between 5,000 and 50,000 for a reasonably sized email dataset. Test emails tokenize correctly with out-of-vocabulary words silently dropped.

### Phase 2 — Computing Class-Conditional Probabilities with Laplace Smoothing

**The question this phase answers:** Given word counts per class, how do you compute $P(w \mid C)$ robustly?

**Key decisions:**
- **Maximum likelihood estimate (MLE):**
  $$P_{\text{MLE}}(w \mid C) = \frac{\text{count}(w, C)}{\sum_{w'} \text{count}(w', C)}$$
  This is the fraction of words in class $C$ that are word $w$. The problem: if `count(w, C) = 0` for some word $w$, then $P_{\text{MLE}}(w \mid C) = 0$, which drives $\prod P(w_i \mid C) = 0$ for any email containing $w$. One unseen word makes the entire class probability zero — the "zero-frequency problem."
- **Laplace smoothing (add-one smoothing):**
  $$P_{\text{Laplace}}(w \mid C) = \frac{\text{count}(w, C) + \alpha}{N_C + \alpha \cdot |V|}$$
  where $N_C = \sum_{w'} \text{count}(w', C)$ is the total word count in class $C$ and $|V|$ is the vocabulary size. $\alpha = 1$ is "add-one"; any $\alpha > 0$ is valid ("add-$\alpha$"). Smoothing shifts probability mass from seen words to unseen words — it acts as a uniform Dirichlet prior on word probabilities.
- **Choosing $\alpha$:** $\alpha = 1$ is the standard default. For large vocabularies and small datasets, $\alpha < 1$ (e.g., 0.1) prevents over-smoothing (taking too much mass from high-frequency words). $\alpha$ can be treated as a hyperparameter and tuned via cross-validation.

**Trap:** Smoothing with the vocabulary size at inference time rather than training time. The denominator must use the vocabulary size fixed at training. At inference, out-of-vocabulary words are ignored — they don't contribute to the log-probability. If you recalculate the denominator based on test vocabulary, your probabilities don't sum to 1 over the training vocabulary.

**Done when:** `log_prob_word_given_class[word][class]` is defined for all vocabulary words in all classes. Verify: `sum(exp(log_prob[w][C]) for w in vocab)` ≈ 1.0 for each class (within floating-point tolerance).

### Phase 3 — Log-Space Classification

**The question this phase answers:** Why must you sum in log-space, and how do you implement it without underflow?

**Key decisions:**
- **Why logs:** A typical email has 100–500 words. If each $P(w_i \mid C) \approx 0.001$, then $\prod_{i=1}^{200} 0.001 = 10^{-600}$ — far below `float64` minimum (`~5e-324`). The product underflows to exactly 0.0 in IEEE 754, making all classes equally probable. Log converts multiplication to addition: $\sum_{i=1}^{200} \log(0.001) = -1380$ — a perfectly representable float.
- **Full log-posterior:**
  $$\log P(C \mid \mathbf{x}) \propto \log P(C) + \sum_{i=1}^{n} \log P(w_i \mid C)$$
  $\log P(C) = \log(N_C / N_{\text{total}})$ is the log-prior. Compare this value across classes; the higher wins.
- **Repeated words:** An email with "free" appearing 5 times adds `5 * log P("free" | C)` to the log-posterior. This is the multinomial Naive Bayes model — it counts word occurrences, not just presence/absence. Bernoulli Naive Bayes would only count presence (0 or 1). Multinomial is generally better for text.

**Trap:** Recomputing `log_prob_word_given_class` inside the prediction loop. These values are fixed after training. Compute them once, store in a dict, look up at inference. Recomputing per-email scales as $O(|V| \cdot N_{\text{test}})$ instead of $O(|V|)$ training + $O(\text{email length} \cdot N_{\text{test}})$ inference.

**Done when:** `predict("FREE MONEY WIN NOW CLICK HERE")` returns `spam`. `predict("The meeting is scheduled for 3pm")` returns `ham`. Log-posteriors are finite (not `-inf` or `nan`).

---

### Phase 4 — Evaluation: Precision, Recall, F1

**The question this phase answers:** Why is accuracy alone insufficient for spam classification, and which metric should you optimize?

**Key decisions:**
- **Class imbalance:** Real spam datasets are often 20–40% spam. A classifier that labels everything as "ham" achieves 60–80% accuracy. Precision and recall expose this failure: that classifier has recall = 0 for spam and precision = undefined.
- **Precision vs. recall tradeoff:** For spam classification, false positives (ham labeled as spam) are the costly error — the user loses legitimate email. False negatives (spam labeled as ham) are merely annoying — the user sees some spam. This cost asymmetry means you should optimize for precision over recall. F1 is a neutral geometric mean; you should report both precision and recall and let the user decide the threshold.
- **Threshold adjustment:** Naive Bayes outputs log-posteriors. The default decision rule is `argmax` — assign to the class with higher log-posterior. But you can adjust the threshold: require that log P(spam) exceed log P(ham) by at least $\delta$ before labeling spam. Higher $\delta$ = higher precision, lower recall. This is how spam filters are tuned in practice.

**Trap:** Computing precision/recall using `sklearn.metrics` without understanding what they compute. Know the confusion matrix entries: TP (spam predicted spam), FP (ham predicted spam), FN (spam predicted ham), TN (ham predicted ham). Precision = TP / (TP + FP). Recall = TP / (TP + FN). F1 = $2 \cdot P \cdot R / (P + R)$.

**Done when:** Evaluation prints a 4-entry confusion matrix and per-class precision/recall/F1. For a good implementation on 20 Newsgroups (talk.politics.misc vs. sci.space, for example), F1 should exceed 0.90.

## Data Flow

```
Raw email strings (train split)
         │
         ▼
tokenize(text) → list of words
         │
         ▼
build_vocabulary(train_tokens, min_count=2)
         │
         ├──────────────────────┐
         ▼                      ▼
count(word, spam_emails)   count(word, ham_emails)
         │                      │
         ▼                      ▼
P(w|spam) with Laplace     P(w|ham) with Laplace
         │                      │
         ▼                      ▼
log_prob_spam[word]        log_prob_ham[word]
         │
         ▼
For each test email:
  log_posterior_spam = log P(spam) + Σ log_prob_spam[w]
  log_posterior_ham  = log P(ham)  + Σ log_prob_ham[w]
  prediction = argmax(spam, ham)
         │
         ▼
Confusion matrix → Precision, Recall, F1, Accuracy
```

## Edge Cases to Handle Before You Code

1. **Empty email after tokenization:** An email that is only punctuation or numbers produces an empty token list. Log-posterior reduces to just the class prior. The classifier assigns the majority class — which is correct behavior. Do not crash on empty token lists; return the prior-argmax prediction.

2. **All-spam or all-ham class:** If the training split by chance contains only one class, `log P(C)` for the other class is `-inf`. This crashes the classifier on any test email. Assert that both classes have at least one training sample before fitting.

3. **Unknown smoothing factor:** If $\alpha = 0$, Laplace smoothing collapses to MLE and you get zero probabilities. Assert `alpha > 0` at construction time with a meaningful error message.

4. **Vocabulary mismatch:** Words in test emails that were excluded by the minimum-count cutoff are unknown and silently skipped. Document this behavior — it means a very unusual email (all rare words) will classify based on the prior alone, which may surprise users.

5. **Very long emails:** A 10,000-word email accumulates 10,000 log-probability terms. Even in log-space, this can produce log-posteriors of $-10^4$ in magnitude. The argmax comparison still works correctly, but if you try to convert back to probabilities via `softmax`, floating-point precision is still challenged. Keep results in log-space for comparison; only exponentiate when you need human-readable probabilities.

## The Hard Design Decisions

| Decision | Option A | Option B | When to pick A |
|----------|----------|----------|----------------|
| Word model | Multinomial (count words) | Bernoulli (word present/absent) | Multinomial: better for longer documents where word frequency carries signal |
| Smoothing | Laplace ($\alpha=1$) | Lidstone ($\alpha<1$, tuned) | Laplace: correct default; Lidstone when vocabulary is large (>100K) and dataset is small |
| Vocabulary | All training words | Min-count cutoff | Min-count cutoff: removes rare noise tokens that consume memory without contributing signal |
| Threshold | argmax (50% decision boundary) | Tuned $\delta$ threshold | Tuned threshold: when false positive and false negative costs are asymmetric (always the case in spam) |

## What Makes This Non-Trivial

The zero-frequency problem is not merely a numerical annoyance — it reveals a fundamental limitation of MLE: estimates from finite data have infinite uncertainty at unseen events, which is philosophically wrong (zero probability means "impossible," not "unseen"). Laplace smoothing is a pragmatic Bayesian fix, but it introduces a hyperparameter ($\alpha$) whose optimal value depends on the vocabulary size and the dataset. The naive independence assumption, meanwhile, is violated so badly for email text (adjacent words co-occur systematically) that a theorist would predict the classifier to fail — but in practice, even with completely wrong independence assumptions, the classifier achieves competitive accuracy because the labels are determined by a few strongly discriminative words (like "FREE", "CLICK", "WIN") that dominate the log-sum regardless of how wrong the joint probabilities are. Understanding *why* a technically incorrect model succeeds is as important as implementing a correct one.

## Interview Angles This Project Develops

1. "Explain why you use log-probabilities in Naive Bayes." — Strong answer: Products of probabilities across many words underflow IEEE 754 `float64` to exactly 0.0 when there are more than ~300 words. Log converts the product to a sum, which remains numerically stable. The argmax decision is unchanged because log is monotone.

2. "What is Laplace smoothing and why is it necessary?" — Strong answer: It adds a pseudocount $\alpha$ to every word's observed count before normalizing. Without it, any word not seen in training for a given class gives that class zero probability for *any* email containing that word, regardless of all other evidence. Laplace smoothing is the maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimate with a uniform Dirichlet prior with concentration $\alpha$.

3. "The naive independence assumption is clearly violated for text. Why does Naive Bayes still work?" — Strong answer: For classification, you only need to correctly rank classes — not to estimate probabilities accurately. If the discriminative words drive the log-sum, the independence assumption barely matters. The model is miscalibrated (raw probabilities are wrong), but the argmax is usually correct. Calibration can be fixed post-hoc with isotonic regression or Platt scaling.

4. "For spam classification, would you optimize precision or recall?" — Strong answer: Precision — because false positives (legitimate email flagged as spam) destroy trust and can cause real harm (missed job offers, missed security alerts). False negatives (spam in the inbox) are merely annoying. The decision boundary should be shifted to require higher confidence before flagging as spam, which increases precision at the cost of recall.

5. "What is the difference between Bernoulli Naive Bayes and Multinomial Naive Bayes?" — Strong answer: Bernoulli uses binary word presence/absence — each word contributes one term to the log-posterior regardless of how many times it appears. Multinomial counts word occurrences — each occurrence adds another log-probability term. Multinomial is generally better for long documents where frequency carries signal; Bernoulli works better for very short documents or when term frequency is uninformative (e.g., Boolean feature data).
