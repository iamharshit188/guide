# Visual Question Answering System — Thinking Guide

> **Module:** 13 — Multimodal Models · **Difficulty:** Expert · **Type:** THINKING · **Time:** ~7 hours

## The Problem

**Input:** An image (JPEG/PNG) and a natural language question about the image (e.g., "What color is the car?" or "How many people are in the image?").

**Output:** A natural language answer (usually 1–5 words for VQA v2). Evaluated on VQA accuracy: each answer is scored against 10 human-provided answers using soft matching — a predicted answer receives a score of $\min(1, \frac{\text{count of humans who said this answer}}{3})$.

**Constraints:** Architecture: ViT image encoder + GPT-2 or Phi-2 decoder, connected via a learned projection layer. The projection layer maps ViT's patch embeddings ($\mathbb{R}^{197 \times 768}$ for ViT-B/16 on a 224×224 image) into the LLM's token embedding space. Train on VQA v2 (train split: ~443K QA pairs across ~82K images). Report strict accuracy (exact match) and VQA score (soft match).

## Before You Write Any Code: Build The Mental Model

The central challenge in VQA is aligning two heterogeneous representation spaces: the vision encoder's patch-level spatial features and the language model's token-level semantic embeddings. These spaces are trained independently on completely different data with completely different objectives. A ViT trained on ImageNet classification learns features that separate dog from cat; GPT-2 trained on web text learns features that predict the next word in English sentences. Neither objective produces representations that are naturally aligned.

The projection layer is the alignment mechanism. It is a learned function $f_\phi: \mathbb{R}^{N_p \times d_v} \to \mathbb{R}^{N_p \times d_l}$ that maps visual patch tokens to the LLM's embedding dimension. During training, $\phi$ must discover a mapping such that the LLM can "read" visual information. The expressivity of $f_\phi$ (linear vs. MLP vs. cross-attention) determines how much of the visual information the LLM can access.

VQA is not a generation task in the traditional sense — for VQA v2, the answer space is effectively closed (top-3129 answers cover 90%+ of the dataset). This means the architecture can be treated as either a classifier (predict the answer class from a fixed vocabulary) or a generative model (generate the answer autoregressively). The classifier approach is simpler and achieves better VQA score per FLOP; the generative approach is more flexible but requires careful training to avoid verbose predictions that fail VQA's soft-match scoring.

Language bias is the dominant failure mode of VQA systems that the architecture alone cannot address. Many VQA questions have strong linguistic priors: "What sport is being played?" → almost always sports, "Is the person smiling?" → 60% yes. A model can achieve 50–65% VQA accuracy purely from language priors (without looking at the image) on VQA v2. Your architecture must learn to attend to the image — verifying this requires explicitly testing with shuffled or blanked images.

## Decompose It

### Phase 1 — Vision Encoder Integration

**The question this phase answers:** How do you extract visual features that carry the information needed to answer questions?

**Key decisions:**
- **Frozen vs. fine-tuned ViT:** Freezing the ViT prevents catastrophic forgetting of the patch-level spatial representations learned during ImageNet pretraining. ViT's patch embeddings encode texture, shape, and spatial relationships at the patch level — the precise representations needed for VQA. Fine-tuning the ViT on VQA's classification objective will shift these representations toward "which features help predict VQA answers" and away from general visual understanding. Unless you have a very large VQA dataset (>10M samples), freeze the ViT.
- **Which ViT features to use:** ViT-B/16 with a 224×224 image produces 196 patch tokens + 1 CLS token = 197 tokens, each 768-dimensional. The CLS token captures global image semantics; patch tokens capture local spatial information. For VQA, spatial information matters ("which object is on the left?") — use all 197 tokens, not just CLS. Ablation: compare CLS-only vs. all-patch — all-patch should improve by 3–5 points on spatial questions.
- **Preprocessing:** ViT expects images normalized with ImageNet mean/std: mean=(0.485, 0.456, 0.406), std=(0.229, 0.224, 0.225). Resize to 224×224, center crop for evaluation. During training, apply random crop and horizontal flip (but NOT rotation — VQA questions often reference orientation: "left," "right," "top").
- **Handling variable-resolution images:** VQA v2 images range from 100×100 to 4000×4000. Naively resizing all to 224×224 distorts aspect ratios and loses information. Better: pad to square, then resize. Or: use a tiled approach for large images — split into 224×224 tiles, process each through ViT, average the CLS tokens. Tiling adds compute but preserves high-resolution detail for questions about small objects.

**Trap:** Using ViT's pre-softmax classification head output (single 1000-dim vector) as the image representation. This collapses all spatial information into a class prediction — useless for spatial VQA questions. Always use the pre-head patch embeddings, accessed by calling `model.forward_features(image)` or removing the classification head.

**Done when:** For a batch of 4 images, ViT produces patch embeddings of shape (4, 197, 768). The CLS tokens for different images have cosine similarity < 0.9 (they represent different images, not the same representation). The embeddings change meaningfully between a photo of a dog and a photo of a car — verify with `torch.nn.functional.cosine_similarity(emb_dog, emb_car).mean()` < 0.7.

### Phase 2 — Projection Layer Design

**The question this phase answers:** How do you connect visual patch tokens to the LLM's embedding space?

**Key decisions:**
- **Linear projection:** $f_\phi(x) = xW$ where $W \in \mathbb{R}^{768 \times 768}$ (for GPT-2) or $W \in \mathbb{R}^{768 \times 2560}$ (for Phi-2). 589K parameters. Fast, stable training, but linear — it cannot represent non-linear mappings between visual and linguistic feature spaces.
- **Two-layer MLP projector:** $f_\phi(x) = \text{GELU}(xW_1)W_2$ where $W_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{768 \times 1536}$, $W_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{1536 \times 768}$. ~3.5M parameters. This is the LLaVA-style projector — empirically better than linear, especially for spatial relationships and attribute binding.
- **Q-Former (BLIP-2 style):** A cross-attention module with $M=32$ learned query tokens that attend to the 197 patch tokens and produce 32 output tokens. The LLM receives only 32 tokens regardless of image size. Pros: reduces visual token count (lower LLM context usage). Cons: 100M+ parameters, training instability, requires a two-stage training procedure. Overkill for a VQA fine-tuning project.
- **Token count trade-off:** Linear/MLP projectors pass all 197 patch tokens to the LLM, using 197 of the LLM's context window. GPT-2's context is 1024 tokens — 197 for image + 50 for question + 10 for answer = 257 tokens, well within budget. For longer-context use cases (image + long document), reducing token count via Q-Former matters more.

**Trap:** Projecting only the CLS token and inserting it as a single "image token" into the LLM's sequence. This works for image captioning (global description) but fails for spatial VQA questions that require reasoning about specific image regions. Always project all patch tokens.

**Done when:** The projector maps (batch, 197, 768) → (batch, 197, LLM_dim). The projected tokens have a similar norm to the LLM's learned word embeddings (check: `projected_tokens.norm(dim=-1).mean()` should be similar to `lm.get_input_embeddings().weight.norm(dim=-1).mean()`). If the norms are off by 10x, the LLM's attention mechanism will be dominated by whichever input is larger.

### Phase 3 — Input Sequence Construction and Training

**The question this phase answers:** How does the model see both image and question during a forward pass?

**Key decisions:**
- **Sequence layout:** The input to the LLM is a concatenation of: (1) image token embeddings (projected patch tokens, shape: 197 × d), (2) question token embeddings (tokenized question, variable length), (3) answer token embeddings (during training). The LLM processes this as a single causal sequence.
- **Attention mask:** Image tokens should attend to each other and to later tokens. Question tokens should attend to image tokens and other question tokens. Answer tokens attend to everything. This is the standard causal mask — no custom masking needed, since the image tokens come first.
- **Loss masking:** During training, compute cross-entropy loss only on answer tokens. The model must not be penalized for "predicting" the question from the image — that's not the task. Implement with a `labels` tensor where image and question token positions are set to -100 (PyTorch's ignore_index for CrossEntropyLoss).
- **Answer format for generative training:** For VQA v2 with the generative approach, format answers as a single word or short phrase followed by EOS. "What color is the ball? → blue." During inference, generate until EOS or max 10 tokens, then strip punctuation for VQA soft-match scoring.
- **Training stages:** Stage 1: train only the projection layer (ViT and LLM frozen). This aligns the two spaces without disturbing either's pre-learned features. Stage 2: optionally unfreeze the LLM's last 2–4 transformer layers for task-specific adaptation. Stage 1 alone achieves ~55–60% VQA score with a pretrained ViT and GPT-2; Stage 2 pushes to ~65–68%.

**Trap:** Computing loss on all token positions including the question. The LLM will learn to predict question words from image patches — a spurious task that competes with answer generation. Always mask the loss to answer tokens only.

**Done when:** A forward pass on a batch of (image, question, answer) triples produces a loss that decreases over 100 training steps. The loss on image and question tokens is exactly 0 (correctly masked). The loss on answer tokens starts near log(vocab_size) and decreases.

### Phase 4 — VQA Accuracy Metric and Bias Analysis

**The question this phase answers:** What does the model actually know — and what does it answer correctly for the wrong reason?

**Key decisions:**
- **VQA score computation:** For each predicted answer $\hat{y}$ and the set of 10 human answers $\{y_1, \ldots, y_{10}\}$: score = $\min(1, \frac{\text{count}(\hat{y} \in \{y_1,\ldots,y_{10}\})}{3})$. This rewards answers that multiple humans gave without requiring exact consensus. A predicted answer that 3+ humans gave scores 1.0; one that 1 human gave scores 0.33.
- **Yes/no imbalance check:** In VQA v2, ~45% of questions are yes/no binary questions, and among those, ~55% have "yes" as the answer. A model that always predicts "yes" achieves 25% VQA accuracy (55% of 45%). Check: what is your model's accuracy on yes/no questions specifically? What fraction of its predictions are "yes"? If > 60%, the model may have learned a yes bias.
- **Language bias detection:** Test the model with a blank (all-gray) image. Record its answers. If the answers are similar to those with the real image, the model is using language priors, not visual features. The VQA v2 dataset was specifically designed to reduce language bias (by including complementary question pairs: same question, different image, different answer), but language bias still dominates in many architectures.
- **Question category breakdown:** Report accuracy separately for: yes/no questions, number questions (answers are digits), and open-ended questions. Strong models should have: yes/no > 85%, number > 45%, open-ended > 50%. A model with good yes/no but poor open-ended accuracy is relying on binary priors.

**Trap:** Evaluating only on strict exact-match accuracy. VQA's soft-match metric is the official metric and is more informative. "Grey" vs. "gray" is a correct prediction under soft match (humans annotate both); strict match would fail it. Always report VQA score, not just exact match.

**Done when:** The evaluation loop produces: VQA score (soft), exact-match accuracy, breakdown by question type (yes/no, number, open), language-bias score (VQA score with blank image vs. real image), and a yes-prediction-rate statistic.

### Phase 5 — Compositional Reasoning Failure Analysis

**The question this phase answers:** Where does the model fail, and why — visual encoding limitation or reasoning limitation?

**Key decisions:**
- **Object detection vs. relationship reasoning:** ViT encodes objects well (a patch containing a red ball activates "red," "ball," "round" features) but poorly encodes relationships between objects ("is the red ball left of the blue cube?"). This is the compositional reasoning gap. Test specifically with spatial relationship questions — these should show the largest accuracy drop.
- **Attribute binding failures:** "What color is the small ball?" requires binding "color" to "small ball" specifically, not to any ball in the image. Multi-object scenes with multiple balls of different colors expose attribute binding failures. The model may answer with the most salient color regardless of the "small" qualifier.
- **Visual grounding gap:** The model can answer "What sport is being played?" correctly (from language bias: the question implies a sport exists, and common sports are in the training data) without actually processing the image. A model that achieves 70% on sport questions with blank images has a visual grounding gap — it is not grounded in the image.
- **Diagnostic test set:** Construct 20 adversarial examples: (a) swapped image pairs — same question, similar images with different correct answers; (b) blank image; (c) spatially ambiguous questions. Performance on these adversarial examples isolates genuine visual understanding from language bias.

**Trap:** Interpreting high VQA accuracy as genuine visual understanding. A model achieving 65% VQA score may achieve 55% with blank images — meaning only 10 percentage points are actually image-dependent. This is the visual grounding gap, and it is not visible from the overall accuracy number alone.

**Done when:** The failure analysis produces: accuracy on spatial relationship questions, accuracy on attribute binding questions (multi-object scenes), accuracy with blank images (language bias baseline), and 5 specific failure examples with diagnostic commentary (wrong for which reason: language bias, object detection failure, or relationship reasoning failure).

## Edge Cases to Handle Before You Code

1. **Image is grayscale (1 channel), but ViT expects 3 channels:** Convert with `image = image.convert("RGB")` before processing. Do not stack the single channel 3 times blindly — some grayscale images (medical imaging, satellite) have different intensity distributions than RGB. For VQA v2 (natural images), RGB conversion is correct.

2. **Answer is a number with multiple valid formats:** "2", "two", "2.0" are all valid answers for a count question. VQA's soft-match treats these as correct if any human annotator used that form. During post-processing, normalize numbers: convert "two" → "2" using a lookup table of number words up to 20. This raises number-question accuracy by 3–5 points.

3. **Very long questions exceed LLM context window:** A 197-token image sequence + 100-token question + 10-token answer = 307 tokens for GPT-2's 1024-token context. This is fine. But if using a smaller context model or very long questions, truncate the question from the end (preserve the first tokens — the question word "what/where/how" at the start matters more than trailing context).

4. **VQA v2 images are not locally available (download required):** The VQA v2 dataset images come from MSCOCO — 25 GB download. For development, use the VQA v2 validation set images only (5K images, ~5 GB). The training images are needed for training — provide a download script rather than assuming they exist.

## The Hard Design Decisions

| Decision | Option A | Option B | When to pick A |
|---|---|---|---|
| Generative vs. classifier head | Generative: LLM generates answer tokens | Classifier: linear head over LLM hidden state predicts answer class | Classifier for higher VQA accuracy per FLOP on VQA v2 (closed answer space); generative for open-ended VQA or novel answer types |
| Frozen vs. fine-tuned ViT | Freeze ViT, train only projector (+ optional LLM layers) | Fine-tune full ViT on VQA | Freeze for small datasets (<100K images); fine-tune only with large VQA datasets and with a low learning rate (1e-5) to avoid destroying spatial features |
| All patch tokens vs. CLS only | All 197 patch tokens → 197 visual tokens to LLM | CLS only → 1 visual token to LLM | All patches for spatial questions; CLS only if context window is tight or question is about global image semantics (captioning) |
| VQA soft-match vs. exact match | Soft match: score = min(1, human_count/3) | Exact match: 1 if predicted == any human answer | Always use soft match for official VQA evaluation; use exact match only for debugging |

## What Makes This Non-Trivial

The alignment problem between vision and language towers is not solved by any single architectural choice — it is managed by the interplay of projection layer capacity, training data scale, and stage-wise training. A linear projector trained on 100K examples can achieve surprisingly high accuracy if the ViT and LLM are both high-quality pretrained models; but it will fail on compositional questions where the relationship between patches matters, not just the content of individual patches.

Language bias in VQA is not a data artifact that can be fully corrected by dataset design. VQA v2 was specifically built to counter language bias by requiring complementary question pairs. Yet models still achieve 55–65% accuracy without looking at images. The bias is in the LLM's priors about how language and real-world statistics co-occur. Correcting it requires either: training with image-shuffled pairs (same question, different image, penalize language-only answers), or architectural changes that force the answer to depend on the image (e.g., requiring the image tokens' attention weights to be non-trivial).

VQA accuracy of 65–70% is achievable with a well-implemented ViT + MLP projector + GPT-2 system. Reaching 80%+ requires: (a) a stronger LLM (Phi-2, LLaMA-7B), (b) pre-training the projector on image captioning data before VQA fine-tuning, (c) larger image resolution (448×448 vs. 224×224), and (d) instruction tuning that teaches the model to "look before answering."

## Interview Angles This Project Develops

1. **"How do you connect a vision encoder to a language model?"** — Strong answer describes the projection layer, explains why norms must be aligned, distinguishes linear vs. MLP vs. Q-Former trade-offs, and knows that all patch tokens outperform CLS-only for spatial reasoning.

2. **"Why should the ViT be frozen during VQA fine-tuning?"** — Strong answer explains catastrophic forgetting: ViT's patch-level spatial representations are learned from ImageNet with millions of images; fine-tuning on VQA's 443K images with a classification objective will overwrite these general features with VQA-specific ones that don't transfer to novel images.

3. **"What is language bias in VQA, and how do you detect it?"** — Strong answer explains that LLMs have priors over answer distributions (sport questions → sports answers), describes the blank-image test, and knows that VQA v2's complementary pairs reduce but do not eliminate this bias.

4. **"What is the VQA soft-match metric and why not use exact match?"** — Strong answer explains the 10-human-annotation scheme, the min(1, count/3) scoring, and that exact match undervalues correct paraphrases (grey/gray, 2/two) by treating them as wrong.

5. **"What does compositional reasoning failure look like in a VQA model, and what causes it?"** — Strong answer distinguishes object detection (ViT handles this well) from relationship reasoning (ViT's patch-independent encoding misses spatial relationships between patches), gives "is the red ball left of the blue cube" as the canonical failure mode, and explains that Q-Former-style cross-attention between patches partially addresses this.
